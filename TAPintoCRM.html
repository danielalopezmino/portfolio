Bringing Clarity to Everyday CRM Work


Product: TAPinto CRM
Role: UX/UI Designer
Users: Local town editors (non-technical users)
Context: Internal platform / Legacy replacement
Environment: Distributed stakeholders, real users, technical constraints

Before designing TAPinto’s custom CRM, the platform utilized HubSpot for marketing and contact management. However, there was a disconnect: while HubSpot is a robust enterprise tool, it was never fully adopted at scale for the network's core revenue driver—local ad sales.

TAPinto’s sustainability relies on selling ads to local businesses. Yet, the work is done by town editors, not dedicated sales teams.

The friction was clear:
- HubSpot’s sales-heavy logic created a high cognitive load for editors who just needed to track local sponsors.
- The tool was built for complex commercial funnels, not the lean, fast-paced reality of local news.
- Because the tool felt "foreign" to their daily routine, it remained underutilized despite the critical need to manage ad revenue.

Problems with the Existing Setup

From the editors’ perspective:
- The interface felt complex, overwhelming, and unintuitive
- Fear of making mistakes led to avoidance of key features
- Many functions were unused due to lack of confidence

From a business perspective:
- Low adoption among town editors
- Limited scalability as the network grew
- The issue was not a lack of features, but a misalignment between the CRM and how editors actually work.

Research & Validation

Before proposing any design solution, it was critical to validate whether the problem was related to training, resistance to change, or the tool itself.

A research survey was conducted with local town editors to understand:
- How they actually used (or avoided) the CRM
- Their main frustrations and fears
- Their level of technical confidence

“This feels like a tool for large companies.”
“I’m afraid of breaking something.”

Key Insights

The research confirmed that the issue was not adoption, but a structural mismatch between the tool and its users:
- Editors perceived the CRM as “designed for large companies, not local newsrooms”
- Complexity generated insecurity and avoidance
- Training did not solve the core usability issues

These insights supported the decision to move away from a generic CRM and design a custom tool tailored to non-technical users and real daily workflows.

Design Starting Point

This context became the foundation for all design decisions that followed. The challenge was not to simplify an existing CRM, but to design a tool that matched how editors actually think and work.

From this point on, the focus shifted from managing contacts in a generic system to designing a clear, safe, and scalable editorial experience.

Core Workflow · Contact Management

The core workflow focused on enabling editors to manage contacts confidently and efficiently, without overwhelming them with unnecessary options.

Design decisions included:
- Clear hierarchy of primary actions
- Editorial language instead of CRM jargon
- Progressive disclosure of advanced fields
- Consistent patterns across views

Activity Tracking & Transparency

To support collaboration and accountability, the CRM included a clear and accessible activity log. Instead of hiding complexity, the goal was to organize it in a way that felt understandable and safe for non-technical users.

Time-Based Features · Notifications & Agenda

As the network scaled, editors needed support to manage timing, follow-ups, and reminders without relying on external tools. Notifications and agenda features were designed to integrate naturally into daily workflows, reducing cognitive load and manual effort.

System Overview

The dashboard and contacts index were designed to provide clarity at a glance, helping editors understand what required attention without feeling overwhelmed.

Design System & Technical Alignment

To ensure consistency, scalability, and technical feasibility, the team adopted Ant Design as the foundation of the CRM’s visual system.

Aligned with this decision, I reconstructed and adapted Ant Design’s core components in Figma (tables, forms, modals, buttons, pagination, tabs, etc.), building a component library that matched the development stack.

This approach allowed us to:

- Design highly realistic prototypes.
- Reduce friction between design and development.
- Ensure visual consistency across the entire platform.
- Think in terms of systems rather than isolated screens.

Key Learnings

Powerful tools are not always usable tools
Designing for non-technical users requires questioning inherited complexity
Research-driven decisions are critical when replacing enterprise tools
Clarity and confidence matter more than feature richness
